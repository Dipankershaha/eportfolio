Rationale

Why these, why this order

I created this based on the seven Graduate Standards, not a compilation of resources, because a collection of worksheets isn't of much value alone. It's what each tool actually does in a classroom and the standards provide a shape which will bear examination.

The main placement I did was Stage 4 and Stage 5 – a class of Year 7 students with a number of EAL/D students. I learned more about scaffolding language and sequencing content in that class than in any other class this year and those are the very things that Stage 6 requires when content is increased in difficulty and there is less room for error. Some of these are directly from that placement. This was a couple I created for this portfolio, because I didn't want you to take the transfer on faith, I wanted to have some real evidence of planning at the Stage 6 level.

All of the following are handouts I would give a head of department on day one, not a coursework assignment. Tap on each of them to open.

Learning goals

Five goals guiding this portfolio

Specific, measurable, related to Stage 6 syllabus and to the Graduate Standards. They are referred to again in the evidence below.

GOAL 1 · STANDARD 2.1

Content knowledge

Consolidate further their understanding of senior content, functions, exponentials and logs, statistics, so that I can design a sequence of Stage 6 studies based on this material without relying on the syllabus in the textbook for each step.

GOAL 2 · STANDARDS 1.5, 3.3

Pedagogy & differentiation

Continue to embed and maintain strategies that are effective with EAL/D students and other students who require additional scaffolding to help them in the lesson, rather than “bolstered” onto the end.

GOAL 3 · STANDARDS 5.1, 6.1

Assessment & reflection

Identify areas of difficulty during the lesson and become more adept at capturing what went well so that I can next time.

GOAL 4 · STANDARDS 1.1, 4.1, 7.3

Standards alignment

Make the Graduate Standards part of my day-to-day planning and practice, not items I review at the conclusion of placement.

GOAL 5 · STANDARD 2.6

Technology integration

When a tool such as GeoGebra or a spreadsheet is used because it allows students to see something they could not see otherwise, use it – don't use it simply to put pretty.

The Word Wall
Vocabulary Supports for a Year 7 EAL/D classroom
1.31.5

Designed for a Year 7 class with a large proportion of students learning both maths and English. It was not a problem with the mathematics, it was a problem with the words "factor", "multiple", and "product". Below the way a student would on the wall poster in my room, click on a term.

Try it · click any card to flip

Why it sits under 1.3 and 1.5

Each term on this wall was not a glossary term, but actually an actual term a student used incorrectly! Because that is not just a worksheet, but 1.3 evidence: it's developed around the language contexts that are present in the room. It is 1.5 evidence because if students did not need it they could not pay attention to it, if they did need it, they could lean on it without being singled out.

What the research says

Mathematics has technical language which does not easily transfer across to the language of everyday conversation and a recent review identified that students who are struggling with mathematics specifically were better served by teaching technical vocabulary explicitly than to learn through incidental exposure (Stevens et al., 2025).

Looking back

It changed my behaviour regarding language. The idea of colour coding terms on the board garnered more hands up than I would have anticipated. As I enter into Stage 6 standard, and the language load does not go away, I am carrying it forward.

Logarithm Lab
A Stage 6 bridge from index laws to logs, built on GeoGebra-style visualisation
2.12.6

Here is the lesson I created as I transitioned an index laws sequence from Stage 5 to Stage 6 Mathematics Advanced. The most difficult concept for students to grasp is that the use of a logarithm is not a new operation, it is simply asking the question, “what power do I need?” but in reverse. Drag the slider below as I have students do it in the GeoGebra version.

Try it · drag the base, watch both curves move

Why it sits under 2.1 and 2.6

The content knowledge here is the bridge itself, treating logs as something that grows out of index laws rather than a topic dropped on students cold. That's 2.1. The slider is not just decoration – it's the only way I have discovered to get students to see the inverse relationship move – in practice not theory.

What the research says

The results of this 2023 meta study (20 years of GeoGebra research) showed that students who were able to manipulate and compare representations with GeoGebra achieved significantly greater achievement gains compared to students who were not.

Looking back

Originally I used to teach index laws as a closed topic, complete and move on. When I created this, I realised that it wasn't really the new idea that was difficult, it was simply not being used as much as I thought it was.

Dice Pool, Round Two
The concepts of probability and relative frequency extended to expected value in Stage 5.
2.1

Rewriting the same die-rolling activity to ask for more work: what is the relative frequency of that event as an estimation of the probability; the law of large numbers and a first look at expected value. The entire interactive version of this one exists in Standard 5, with the formative assessment angle being more important.

Why it sits under 2.1

It's the same physical action, enhanced! The aim at Stage 5 was to get comfortable by collecting and reading data. The point about content knowledge is to go deeper into the topic without reinventing it, and that's what I'm asking about that same data at Stage 6.

What the research says

The fewer pieces of new information students must retain the first time they attempt an activity they have seen in the model, the more likely they are to succeed (Chen et al., 2021).

Looking back

In Stage 5, some of the students did 100% success in this and still could not explain why their results were not the same as the theoretical value. The Stage 6 version doesn't let that comparison pass without being made.

Interest Calculator
Compound interest, hand-calculated first, then modelled
3.33.4

A stage 6, standard 2 unit of work about compound interest, based on a real-life scenario – saving money for a car. Students model it on their own for a few periods, then they transition to this spreadsheet model. Replace the numbers below with those a student will use.

Try it · change principal, rate, or years

Why it sits under 3.3 and 3.4

On purpose, I get students to do manual calculations before using the model, and that's only when they know what it's automating. The teaching strategy is that ordering, 3.3. The decision not to use a generic formula sheet was a resourcing decision to allow students to see growth over years, so it is 3.4.

What the research says

A random experiment on secondary students demonstrated that financial education that is based on real scenarios and not abstract formulas can lead to measurable improvements in financial knowledge and confidence (Zhu et al., 2021).

Looking back

The only lesson here that I had to build from scratch was this one (no Stage 4 or 5 version to work with) I have stuck with the worked example table as it really has supported pupils' working all year.

Pace Check
What I changed after watching students check out mid-lesson
4.14.3

It's a reflection I wrote for my mentor after a series of Year 8 Numeracy probability lessons with 2 problems that would continually arise: Students checking out during longer explanations and I leaning on slides. Click the arrows below to view the actual pacing change.

Try it · toggle before and after

A single 14-minute explanation block, followed by independent practice.

Why it sits under 4.1 and 4.3

It's 4.1 because I did specific adjustments to make it so that more students were doing something in the lesson, rather than just listening. 4.3 because the routines, consistent consequences, addressing lateness privately, a 90 second limit on individual help were not done on the hoof but were planned and applied in the same manner each time.

What the research says

The duration and continuity of the explanation load can impact the success of an explanation (Chen et al., 2021).

Looking back

This was the first time I took responsibility for a problem in the room, not the class being distracted. Following this lesson, each Stage 6 lesson in this portfolio will be shorter and more frequent due to this lesson.

Dice Pool
The pooled-data check from the Standard 2 probability lesson
5.15.2

The same probability lesson as standard 2, but the formative check is that students share their results from the die roll as a class and compare them to theoretical probability and explain why they are different. Roll several times below the class as they do.

Try it · roll and watch the gap close
Total rolls: 0

Theoretical probability for each face: 1/6 ≈ 16.7%. Watch the bars converge as the roll count grows.

Why it sits under 5.1 and 5.2

5.1 because I can do it live and I can tell, on the spot, which students have an understanding of the difference between experimental and theoretical probability. 5.2 – Feedback is a written explanation that is not evaluated at the end of the lesson.

What the research says

A study in 2023 specifically examining mathematics classrooms reported that formative assessment had a positive effect on achievement, ranging from an approximate effect size of 0.25 to 0.38 based on various implementation of formative assessment.

Looking back

This convinced me formative assessment could not be a homework quiz tacked onto the end of a lesson, but should be integrated into the lesson.

Self-reflection

What this portfolio taught me

WHAT STUCK WITH ME

It's actually building Logarithm Lab that has made me think about Stage 5 content. This was something I used to think of as a chapter that I could close and forget about. I had to make it into a Stage 6 lesson about logs once and then I understood that it was the base for the entire topic.

WHERE I GOT STUCK

Pace Check is essentially me saying I talked too much. I would have my students carefully explain, and then 1/2 of the class would be gone two minutes later. If that was to happen, I would have to cut my explanations short, and trust the students to handle it without me as much as I was used to giving them.

WHERE I’VE ACTUALLY IMPROVED

You can see how I plan sequences now instead of single lessons, particularly in how Logarithm Lab and Watch & Note both go from something concrete to something abstract purposely. I used to avoid using GeoGebra and spreadsheets and made myself look cool and now I use them because students could see something in there which they could not otherwise.

WHAT’S NEXT

The obvious gap is calculus and I haven't done anything in that area and feel the least comfortable on in Stage 6 Advanced I, so I would like to make sure that that is solidified. I wish more of these sorts of checks like Dice Pool, which did the job for me and I've not experienced one since. I want to write Pace Check more often than when it fails badly enough to warrant it, as this is the one I really wish I did more.
