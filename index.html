Rationale

Why these, why this order

I built this around the seven Graduate Standards instead of just listing resources, because a pile of worksheets doesn't tell anyone much on its own. What actually matters is what each piece of work does in a real classroom, and the standards give that a shape that holds up to scrutiny rather than just looking busy. My teaching philosophy comes down to one idea I keep returning to: mathematics only becomes meaningful once a student can see how it connects to something they already understand or something that actually matters to them.

Two pieces of evidence here come directly from a Stage 6 unit of work I designed for Year 11 Mathematics Advanced, Probability and Discrete Probability Distributions, built using the Understanding by Design framework and aligned to the NESA Mathematics Advanced Stage 6 Syllabus. To show planning capability across more topics, I also built four sample lessons spanning Functions, Calculus, Financial Mathematics, and Sequences & Series, each clearly marked as a demonstration piece rather than something delivered in a real classroom. The remaining two artefacts are drawn from my Stage 5 placement, kept because the underlying habits they demonstrate, reading a room and adjusting on the fly, and maintaining honest, structured communication with a mentor, are exactly the capabilities that don't change between stages even when the content does.

I've tagged every artefact by source, real unit, sample lesson, or placement, so the portfolio is honest about what was actually delivered to students versus what was built to demonstrate planning and content knowledge. I'd rather be transparent about that distinction than blur it for the sake of a tidier-looking portfolio. The stat cards above and the badges in the top bar are clickable, jump straight to a group of evidence rather than scrolling through everything.

Know students and how they learn

Focus areas: 1.3 (diverse linguistic, cultural and socio-economic backgrounds), 1.4 (strategies for teaching Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students), 1.5 (differentiate teaching to meet specific learning needs).

Student Diversity Plan
Differentiation built into every lesson of the Probability unit
Stage 6 · MA-S1 Probability UnitProbability
1.31.41.5

This is the actual Student Diversity section from my Year 11 Mathematics Advanced unit of work on Probability and Discrete Probability Distributions. Rather than a single generic differentiation note, it specifies real adjustments for five distinct groups in the same 20-student class.

From the unit document
CohortAdjustment
2 high-ability studentsDerivation of E(X) for the geometric distribution, an introduction to Bayes’ theorem, and the St Petersburg paradox.
2 students with learning difficultiesScaffolded glossary templates, partially completed distribution tables, step-by-step formula guides, calculator access at all times.
1 EAL/D studentKey vocabulary pre-taught before each lesson; visual representations before symbolic notation; bilingual glossary; sentence starters.
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander studentsThe 8ways pedagogy framework underlies the use of real community health data and problem framing through stories, developed in consultation with the school’s Aboriginal Education Consultant.

Why it sits under 1.3, 1.4 and 1.5

These adjustments came from imagining five specific students in one real class, not from a generic differentiation checklist bolted on afterward. That's 1.3 and 1.5 in practice. The ATSI strand goes further than token acknowledgement, using actual community-relevant data and a named pedagogical framework, which is what 1.4 is actually asking for.

What the research says

Universal Design for Learning improves achievement across diverse cohorts, but only when barriers are minimised through proactive design rather than reactive accommodation added after the fact (Almeqdad et al., 2023). For Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students specifically, authentic inclusion requires genuine curriculum connection through community-relevant data and story-based problem framing (O’Keeffe et al., 2023).

Looking back

Writing five separate, specific adjustments instead of one paragraph of generic differentiation language forced me to actually picture each student rather than write what sounds correct. The EAL/D adjustments in particular came straight from what worked with a Year 7 EAL/D student during my Stage 5 placement, just applied to harder content.

Multi-Stage Experiments & the Product Rule
Lesson 1 of the Stage 6 Probability unit, Week 1
Stage 6 · MA-S1 Lesson 1 · Week 1Probability
2.12.5

A full 60-minute Year 11 Mathematics Advanced lesson teaching P(AB) = P(A) × P(B) for independent events, built around a physical spinner demonstration before any formula appears, then formalised through teacher modelling, and connected to a genetics STEM context. Try the simulator below the way students do in the engagement phase.

Try it · spin twice, see if the product rule checks out

Why it sits under 2.1 and 2.5

The lesson sequence, concrete hook, dot diagram, formal rule, independent practice, is built to take students from intuition to formal notation deliberately, which is what 2.1 is asking for. Key vocabulary, trial, event, independent, is pre-taught before the lesson, which is 2.5 in direct practice.

What the research says

Explicit instruction using modelled examples before independent practice establishes correct procedure and reduces cognitive load, with the strongest effects for students with disabilities and those below expected performance levels (Peltier, 2024).

Looking back

Linking the product rule to a genetics scenario, rather than leaving it as an abstract spinner exercise, was a deliberate attempt to make independence feel like something worth understanding rather than a rule to memorise.

Quadratic Functions & Transformations
Sample lesson: vertex form, roots, and graph transformations
Sample Lesson · Demonstration of PlanningFunctions
2.12.6

A Stage 6 Mathematics Advanced lesson on quadratic functions in vertex form, f(x) = a(x − h)2 + k, moving from reading off the vertex and axis of symmetry directly, to finding roots algebraically, to predicting the effect of changing a, h, and k on the graph before checking the prediction. Adjust the sliders below the way students would.

Try it · drag to change a, h, k and watch the parabola move

Worked example, as given to students

For f(x) = −2(x − 1)2 + 8: the vertex is at (1, 8) directly from the formula. Setting f(x) = 0 gives −2(x−1)2 = −8, so (x−1)2 = 4, so x − 1 = ±2, giving roots at x = 3 and x = −1.

Why it sits under 2.1 and 2.6

Teaching transformations through prediction-then-check, rather than rule memorisation, is a content-knowledge and teaching-strategy decision under 2.1. The slider tool is 2.6 in direct use, chosen because static graphs can't show the relationship between changing a parameter and the resulting transformation in real time.

Looking back

Building this confirmed something I'd suspected from the Probability unit: students trust a rule far more once they've seen it fail their own prediction first.

Differentiation & Stationary Points
Sample lesson: finding and classifying turning points of a cubic
Sample Lesson · Demonstration of PlanningCalculus
3.23.3

A lesson sequence on using the derivative to locate and classify stationary points, planned in three explicit stages: differentiate, solve f′(x) = 0, then test either side of each solution to classify it as a maximum, minimum, or neither.

Try it · move along the curve and watch the gradient

Worked example, as given to students

For f(x) = x3 − 3x2 − 9x + 5: f′(x) = 3x2 − 6x − 9 = 3(x − 3)(x + 1). Setting f′(x) = 0 gives x = 3 or x = −1. At x = −1, f′ changes positive to negative, a local maximum, f(−1) = 10. At x = 3, f′ changes negative to positive, a local minimum, f(3) = −22.

Why it sits under 3.2 and 3.3

Splitting "find stationary points" into three separate, practised stages is a sequencing decision under 3.2. The slider tool, letting students watch the gradient change sign in real time, is a teaching strategy choice under 3.3.

Looking back

The classification step is where most errors happen, not the differentiation itself. I'd build in more isolated practice on sign testing before combining it with the rest of the process.

Stage 5 · Professional Experience PlacementPractice
4.14.3

A reflection I wrote for my mentor after a run of Year 8 Numeracy probability lessons, where two things kept going wrong: students checking out during longer explanations, and me leaning too hard on slides. Toggle below to see the actual pacing shift.

Try it · toggle before and after

A single 14-minute explanation block, followed by independent practice.

Why it sits under 4.1 and 4.3

4.1 because the changes I made were specifically about getting more students doing something during the lesson. 4.3 because the routines weren’t improvised, they were decided in advance and applied the same way every time.

What the research says

How much new information a learner has to hold in mind at once affects how well an explanation lands; long, unbroken explanations push that load higher (Chen, Woolcott, & Kalyuga, 2021).

Looking back

This was the first time I traced a problem in the room back to something I was doing, not the class being distracted. Every Stage 6 sample lesson in this portfolio is paced in shorter blocks because of it.

STANDARD 5

Assess, provide feedback and report on student learning

Focus areas: 5.1 (assess student learning), 5.2 (provide feedback to students on their learning).

Compound Interest Assessment Task
Sample lesson: a marked exercise on compound interest, with worked feedback
Sample Lesson · Demonstration of PlanningFinancial Maths
5.15.2

A short formative assessment task on compound interest, A = P(1 + r)n, designed with a marking guide attached so feedback is specific rather than just a tick or a mark out of five.

Try it · change principal, rate, or years

Worked example, as marked

$8,000 is invested at 4.5% p.a. compounded annually for 6 years. A = 8000(1.045)6 = $10,418.08. A student who writes A = 8000 × 1.045 × 6 (treating it as simple interest) gets the substitution mark only, with feedback pointing specifically at the exponent.

Why it sits under 5.1 and 5.2

A marking guide that separates substitution, order of operations, and final answer into distinct marks is what makes 5.1 diagnostic rather than just evaluative. Writing feedback that names the specific error is 5.2 in practice.

Looking back

The simple-interest misconception is common enough that I'd build a deliberate contrast into the lesson before the assessment task.

Arithmetic & Geometric Sequences
Sample lesson: building intuition before introducing the formulas
Sample Lesson · Demonstration of PlanningSequences
6.26.4

Planning this lesson was itself a learning exercise: I deliberately delayed introducing Tn = a + (n−1)d and Sn until after students had generated several sequences by hand and noticed the pattern themselves.

Try it · build a sequence term by term

Worked example, as given to students

Arithmetic: a = 5, d = 3. T20 = 5 + (20−1)(3) = 62. S20 = 670. Geometric: a = 3, r = 2. T10 = 3(2)9 = 1536. S10 = 3069.

Why it sits under 6.2 and 6.4

Reworking my own habitual approach, formula first, then practice, into pattern first, then formula, came directly out of reading current research on inquiry-based mathematics teaching, which is 6.2 in practice. Testing whether the delayed-formula approach actually produced better retention is what 6.4 asks for.

Looking back

I'd want to track this properly with two parallel classes before claiming it works, since my confidence right now is based on classroom impression rather than evidence I've actually collected.

Self-reflection

What this portfolio taught me

WHAT STUCK WITH ME

Writing five separate, specific differentiation adjustments for the Student Diversity Plan, instead of one generic paragraph, forced me to actually picture each student rather than write what sounds correct.

WHERE I GOT STUCK

Pace Check is essentially me admitting I talked too much during placement. I would plan a tight explanation, then watch half the room check out two minutes in. Fixing that meant cutting my own explanations down.

WHERE I'VE ACTUALLY IMPROVED

The shorter, staged structure in Differentiation & Stationary Points is a direct descendant of the Pace Check reflection. My use of technology has shifted too: every slider and calculator in this portfolio exists because it lets students see something they couldn't otherwise.

WHAT'S NEXT

I want to test whether the pattern-first approach in Sequences actually outperforms formula-first teaching, rather than relying on classroom impression. I also want to build a UbD-style backward-planned unit around one of these sample topics the way I did for Probability.
